{{ user.wsc }} Water Science Center Surface Water Quality Assurance Plan

Abstract

This Surface-Water Quality-Assurance Plan documents the standards, policies, and procedures used by the U.S. Geological Survey (USGS) {{ user.wsc }} Water Science Center (WSC) for activites related to the collection, analysis/processing, storage, and publication of surface-water data. Surface-water data include measurements of gage height, velocity, streamflow, suspended-sediment concentration and load, and precipiation.

Introduction

The USGS was established by an act of Congress on March 3, 1879, to provide a permanent Federal agency to perform the systematic and scientific "classification of the public lands, and examination of the geologic structure, mineral resources, and products of the national domain." Surface-water data collection and analysis activities in the USGS are part of the overall mission of appraising the Nation's water resources. Surface-water information, including stage, streamflow, precipitation, suspended-sediment concentration and load data, and basin characteristics are used at the Federal, State, and local levels for resources planning and management.

The purpose of this Surface-Water Quality-Assurance Plan (QA Plan) is to document USGS and {{ user.wsc }} WSC standards, policies, and procedures for activities related to the collection, processing, storage, analysis, and publication of surface-water data. The QA Plan reflects minimum National standards that must be applied consistently across the country as well as higher standards or additional operational guidelines needed to address hydrologic conditions and data operations unique to the {{ user.wsc }} WSC. Elements of the USGS Water Mission Area (WMA) will review the plan periodically to ensure policies and methodologies are kept current. WSC staff with assigned responsibility will review and revise this QA Plan at least once/year to ensure that the plan remains consistent with WSC assigned responsibilities, operational plans, or additional standards as necessary according to the unique staffing and hydrologic conditions of the {{ user.wsc }} WSC.

This plan outlines most required policies and procedures attendant to surface water data programs and identifies personnel responsible for ensuring they are carried out. The plan also serves as a resource for identifying memorandums, publications, and other literature that describe in more detail associated techniques and requirements. There is some redundancy in this report in an attempt to make certain the content of each section stands alone as complete.

The scope of this plan includes discussions of the policies and procedures followed by the USGS for the collection, processing, analysis, storage, and publication of surface-water data. Specific types of surface-water data include stage, velocity, streamflow, suspended-sediment concentration and load, precipitation, and basin characteristics. In addition, issues related to the management of the computer database, employee safety, and technical training are presented.

The water-resource science and monitoring activities of the USGS generally are housed in the USGS WMA. In the past, the WMA was known as the Water Resources Division and Water Resources Discipline, both of which used the abbreviation “WRD”. Many currently valid historical references/citations for policies and publications used in this Plan will still contain the WRD acronym.

At the time of this writing the WMA is undergoing a significant reorganization that includes the dissolution of the Office of Surface Water (OSW) and an independent Water Science Field Team (WSFT) that has historically provided the WMA focus on quality assurance of surface-water activities. The new organization will feature a new Office of Quality Assurance (OQA) and five divisions that will focus on observations, laboratories, Earth science, modeling and prediction, and data dissemination and visualization. WMA quality assurance functions will be coordinated by the new OQA with responsibility for technical support and guidance and technical review vested in various branches of these divisions. As a result, this document is expected to evolve as the new organization takes shape, lines of authorities and linkages are established, and experience is gained.

Responsibilites

Quality assurance (QA) is an active process. Achieving and maintaining high-quality standards for surface-water data are accomplished by specific actions carried out by specific individuals. Errors and deficiencies can occur when individuals fail to carry out their responsibilities. Clear and specific statements of responsibilities promote an understanding of each person's duties in the overall process of assuring surface-water data quality.

Responsibilities are assigned to employees by position title. The responsibility for the preparation of, implementation of, and adherence to the QA policies described in the QA Plan is with the Director of the {{ user.wsc }} WSC. Project Chiefs involved in the collection of surface water data are required to follow the procedures herein or prepare a separate QA plan as part of their proposal.

Safety

Performing work activities in a manner that ensures the safety of personnel and others is of the highest priority for the USGS. Beyond the obvious negative impact unsafe conditions can have on personnel, such as accidents and personal injuries, they also can have a direct effect on the quality of surface-water data and data analysis. For example, errors may be made when an individual’s attention to detail is compromised by dangerous conditions that create distractions or when necessary tasks and maintenance are neglected because of those dangerous conditions. WMA policy memorandums related to safety are located at https://water.usgs.gov/admin/memo/policy/wrdpolicy99.32.html.

Collection of Stage, Velocity and Streamflow Data

Streamgage Installation and Maintanence

Proper installation and maintenance of gaging stations are critical activities for ensuring quality in streamflow-data collection and analysis. Effective site selection, correct design and construction, and regular maintenance of a gage can make the difference between complete records and efficient and accurate determination of streamflow data or missing records and time-consuming, poor quality estimations of streamflow data.

Sites for installation of gaging stations are selected with the intent to meet the purpose of each specific gage. Additionally, sites are selected with the intent of achieving, to the greatest extent possible, ideal hydraulic conditions. Criteria that describe the ideal gaging-station site are listed in Rantz and others (1982, p. 5). These criteria include unchanging natural controls that promote a stable stage-discharge relation, a nearby satisfactory reach for measuring discharge throughout the range of stage, and the means for efficient access to the gage and measuring location. Other aspects of gage installation and maintenance, including stilling wells and pressure transducers, are discussed in Sauer and Turnipseed (2010). All gage heights above the arbitrary gage datum should be referenced to an established datum, such as North American Vertical Datum of 1988 (NAVD88). Methods for establishing a common datum are described in Rydlund and Densmore (2012). Office of Water Information (OWI) Technical Memorandum 2002.01 recommends NAVD88 be the datum used for USGS gaging stations.

Discontinued Gages

Gaging stations may be discontinued due to funding shortfalls or the completion of site-specific studies or data collection. When this occurs, it is the responsibility of the affected office to notify the cooperating agency. At that time, all data acquisition is to cease and the responsible party shall be notified of the gage’s status so that the National Water Information System (NWIS) can be updated. The USGS tracks and reports threatened and endangered streamgage to highlight the loss of every streamgage and the ongoing general need for funding support for the network (URL: https://water.usgs.gov/networks/fundingstability/GuidanceDocs/UpdatingTEDRS.pdf). In addition, for real-time stations, information shall be posted on the discontinued gage’s NWISWeb page notifying the public why the gage has been discontinued and to provide contact information for parties interested in reactivating the gage.

Any discontinued gaging stations must be documented annually in the Discontinued Stations Capital Improvement (DS-CI) database. (WRD Policy Memorandum 09.02). OSW Technical Memorandum 2016.06 establishes a process to support the allocation of DOI facilities funding to facilitate the safe removal of discontinued stations and remove/replace some cableways or other “attractive nuisances” in certain situations. WSCs must submit annual requests for DS-CI funding via the online form listed in the Application Process described in OSW Technical Memorandum 2016.06. Requests are evaluated and funded monthly until the annual funds are expended.

Site Documentation

Station Descriptions

A station description is prepared for each gaging station and becomes part of the permanent record. Station descriptions are written to include specific types of information in a consistent format as illustrated by Kennedy (1983, p. 2). The goal of a well-written station description is to provide an archive of knowledge that has been gained regarding a particular gaging station over time. A critical component of the station description is to clearly identify the primary reference gage; multiple reference gages may be necessary if the range of stage and site conditions dictate it. The range of stage covered by each gage should be provided.

Station descriptions should describe the hydraulic control and its sensitivity and stability at various flow and water levels. Key information includes the gage height of zero flow and the control cross-section breakpoints and transition zones. Station descriptions should also describe the measurement locations, approximate safe wading depth, and list special equipment that may be needed to safely access and measure the stream at various flows.

Photos and Videos

Photographs and videos are taken by the hydrographers for the purpose of documenting gage-house construction, changes in control conditions, floods, damage to gage features, or to supplement various forms of written documentation such as station descriptions. Detailed documentation of site conditions with photographs or videos are also an important part of theoretically-based analyses such as indirect measurement computations, flow computations that use surface-velocity observations, and step-backwater studies. Hydrographers should have access to mobile devices with photo/video capability and have them readily available when servicing gages. Photographs can be attached to a specific site visit in the Field Data Editor of AQUARIUS.
